preva redesign


Overview

Preva is a fitness tracking app specifically designed to help a user reach his or her
fitness goals.

It’s best used for fitness tracking in conjunction with Precor cardio equipment. The app tracks distance, duration and calories for all fitness activities in and outside of the gym such as group classes, sports and recreational activities, and outdoor fitness.

Role

UX and UI design
Entire product design from research to conception, visualization and prototyping


*Disclaimer: this is a conceptual project and not affiliated to Precor.

Challenge

Low rating reviews and majority of users aren’t happy and satisfied with the current app. Some comments from the reviews are ....

Dissapointing, cold and anti social, and frustrating experience.

What is the point of this app?

Completely worthless for all the reasons everyone else is saying.


*As of 12/2019

Research

I read through the user ratings/reviews from Google Play and Apple store. I was able to gather qualitative and quantitative data from actual users. I analyzed the reasons why many users don't like the current app, how they want to use the app and thier purpose of useing the app.




Based on the review research I created an affinity map to sort out users’ opinoins.
I identified top 3 severe pain points.


Paint point #1
Outdated

The app hasn’t been updated for 5 years. The functionality and the look and feel of the app are not being kept up to current trends and needs.

Paint point #2
No integration with external app

Overwhelming amount of users pointed out that the lack of sharing data with other existing fitness apps is a huge disappointment.

Paint point #3
Limited data editing functionality

The app limits users to edit fitness activity data and it causes to display incorrect progress.

There are 2 primary reasons to this.
1) Users aren't allowed to input historical data older than the current day.
2) Users aren't allowed to edit data manually.


In addition, I listed top 5 app and device mentioned in the reviews and ranked based on frequency (how many times the app were mentioned) to prioritize user needs for pain
point #2.

No.1 - Apple Health

Over 20 users out of 148 reviews mentioned.

No.2 - Google Fit

15 users out of 807 reviews mentioned.

No.3 - Fitbit

10 users mentioned in the reviews from both Apple store and google play.

No.4 - Runkeeper

3 users mentioned in the reviews from both Apple store and google play.

No.5 - My Fitness Pal

2 users mentioned in the reviews from both Apple store and google play.

Secondary Research

By going over app reviews I found out that many users are gym members and only few are owners of Precor equipment for personal use.

To familiarize myself about gym members, I researched and immersed myself on fitness and gym trends. This helped me to have a better understanding about fitness app and its user.

Who goes to the gym?

The average gym goer is a little over 40 years old but most members are 25-60 years old.

What gym members actually do at the gym?

50% - claim they go to the gym just to check out the opposite sex.
44% - workout with a partner.
40% - enroll in group exercise like Zumba, yoga, spinning or kickboxing.
12.5% - use a personal trainer.

Walking is the most common activity.

How often do gym members go to the gym?

The average gym member goes to the gym twice a week.

What type of fitness equipment are popular?

Treadmills and strength training machines.

User Personas

Combining actual user data and statistical gym membership data, I created 2 personas who are most likely to present themselves as typical app users.


Competitive Analysis

I tested various different fitness apps and observed how they implemented a data integration functionality. I sketched out the flow of each app and found a pattern.

All of these competitors have one common way which is to create the functionality and placed it under settings page to navigate integration process. It appeared to be de facto standard.


Naming conventions of the functionality are all similar. For instance, Devices and services, App settings, Connect with Health App, and Apps & Devices.

I decided to create a user flow diagram of the integration functionality with Apple Health based on my research to have a better understanding.

Designing Solutions

After gathering all the information I realized that working on the 2 most severe pain points is the most effective way to improve overall performance of the existing app which includes the following:

Pain point #2 - Implementing data integration to external app feature
I chose to implement the most frequently mentioned from the app review. This feature was inevitable to keep up with current trends and satisfy users' needs.

Pain point #3 - Implementing manual data editing feature
I decided to create a page for listing fitness activities and detail page of each activity to let user to edit data feature.


Current architecture

I made wireflows for adding fitness activity feature to understand user flow from the existing app.


Afterwards, I created a sitemap to study further. I discovered some flaws; from recent activity page it navigates to the same add activity page repeatedly. This causes confusion and incorrect data calculation. Also, confusing naming convention and information architecture are other challenges to be tackled.



New architecture

Reorganizing category and planning for new features I made following changes to information architecture.

1) Delete the repetitive content pages.
2) Optimize the naming convention.
3) Add a data integration list from Settings page.
4) Add date section on manual data entry page.
5) Implement edit and save buttons on the single activity page.

Visual Design

Style guide

Before designing the high fidelity wireframes, I created a style tile so I could get a better sense of the UI direction that I will be using for my prototype. I want users to have a visual connection to the brand. So I chose the same color schemes to keep the look and feel consistent. I also decided that the background color for the app is going to be dark to present Precor fitness machine.



Logo

Next, I redesigned a new logo. The shape of the logo was inspired by the existing Precor logo. I created 2 different versions. I wasn’t really sure which one to go with and I planned to conduct a preference test.



Preference test

I completed the preference test using a guerrilla testing method. There were 10 participants, both gym member and non member, including the people I conducted the test with. This gave me clear feedback that the design on the left was more appealing to potential users.

Prototype

Based on the prototype feedback, I redesigned the dashboard layout to ease the confusion. Once I finally felt ready to create the hi-fi wireframes of my proposed solutions, I took my previous sketches and low-fidelity wireframes to develop hi-fi wireframes for my prototype.



Reflection & Next Step

There are lots more for improvement and this is just a beginning. The app only offers simple functionalities in comparison to other big name fitness apps on the market. However, by providing simple and intuitive navigations and features user would be motivated to keep using the app instead of stop using. In the future, by providing more features might benefit to monetizing.

Next step

Test my prototype with both gym goers and non gym goers. I'd like to try some other testing methods than guerrilla testing at the gym.
